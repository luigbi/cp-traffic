Glossary

This is a glossary of terms related to broadcasting and can be used as a reference guide to the different terminology used with the Counterpoint Traffic and Affiliate systems.


  • A/E (account executive): a salesperson.
  • Acquisition costs: fees paid to a station or vendor for acquiring air time inventory or for facilitating or creating NTR items.
  • ADU: audience deficiency unit. Units of commercial advertising inventory made available to advertisers as fulfillment for inventory the advertiser bought that ran in programs that under-delivered on contracted audience ratings.
  • Ad Location: in a podcast, where ads are inserted. Pre-roll (at the beginning), Mid-roll (in the middle), and Post-roll (at the end).
  • Ad Server: a program used to dynamically insert ads into podcasts or other audio. Examples include Megaphone and AdsWizz.
  • Affiliate affidavit: a list of the spots that aired at an affiliate by vehicle, along with the aired dates and times, as certified by the affiliate.
  • Affiliate agreement: an agreement between a network and a station that defines what that station will air for a given vehicle. Agreements have a defined date range - typically from a specified start date until TFN (till further notice).
  • Air time: spots that are sold for airing in broadcast inventory. Not NTR or Merchandising and Promotion.
  • Airing vehicle: a special type of vehicle that produces logs for selling vehicles. Airing vehicles have programming, but do not appear on contracts and are not trafficked. Airing and selling vehicles are linked together on the Links screen.
  • Assign copy: the process in which a specific piece of copy inventory is attached to a spot, using the copy rotation instructions that were entered on the Copy screen.
  • Audience: a count of the number of people listening to a spot.
  • Audio type: a two letter code used to identify the type of commercial a contract line is intended for. The available types are: LC (live commercial), LP (live promo), RP (recorded promo), PC (pre-recorded commercial), PP (pre-recorded promo), and RC (recorded commercial).
  • Automation exports and imports: automation exports can be run from the Traffic system to send Traffic spot information to third party automation systems, and the updated airing information can be imported back to the Traffic system for many of the automation services.
  • Avail: a piece of inventory that spots can be booked into.
  • Average Persons: the number of people that on average will be exposed to each spot. Calculated by multiplying Population by Rating and then dividing by 100.
  • Average Quarter Hour (AQH): a count of the average listening audience for each fifteen minute period, all day long.
  • Away from home listening: an estimate reported for a listening location outside of the home.
  • Baked-in: a podcast ad that is part of the podcast audio file so all listeners hear it. 
  • Band: Typically AM or FM (as in KAAA-FM).
  • Billboard: spots that air at the beginning or end of a program, announcing the names of the advertisers who are sponsoring it. Billboard spots are usually 5 or 10 seconds in length, and merely mention the name of the advertiser. It is implied that one or more full commercials for that advertiser will air inside the program during a regular commercial break. 
  • Billed and Booked report: a Traffic system report that shows an entire year of advertising dollars. Past month figures are obtained from billing, and future month figures are obtained from business on the books.
  • Blackout: copy that airs and replaces the copy for one advertiser with copy from another advertiser, typically for a specific copy region. 
  • Boilerplate: a standardized comment that can be defined and reused.
  • Bonus: spots given as a reward (bonus) to an advertiser.
  • Broadcast calendar (or standard broadcast calendar): a Monday through Sunday calendar where the first week of every month must always contain the 1st day of the month from the Gregorian calendar.
  • Bypassed makegood: when using the affiliate website version 2, a missed spot that has been flagged by the affiliate as a spot that will never be made good.
  • Call Letters: a sequence of characters used to identify a radio station, such as KAAA.
  • Cart: internally assigned copy inventory numbers.
  • Certificate of Performance: a traffic log that includes a place for station personnel to sign off on what aired.
  • Commercial Break: a specific amount of time within a program set aside for commercials.
  • Contract: an agreement between a network and an agency or direct advertiser that specifies the vehicles and dates that will be purchased.
  • Conventional vehicle: a vehicle where the spots sold on that vehicle are scheduled into that vehicle.
  • Copy grid: a feature that allows multiple copy rotations to be entered for a single contract at the same time, rather than one at a time, allowing for a faster copy entry process. 
  • Copy inventory: an actual piece of copy (ISCI and/or Cart) that is tied to an advertiser and product, has a designated length, and may have other information as well such as a creative title.
  • Copy ratio: how often each piece of copy in a given set of rotation instructions must air in order to meet the requirements of a full rotation.
  • Copy region: a group of stations with a commonality that the advertiser is trying to target with unique, region-specific copy.
  • CPM (Cost per Thousand): the amount of money needed to reach each thousand people in the audience.
  • CPP (Cost per Point): the amount of money needed to reach one percent of the audience.
  • Creative title: a title used with copy inventory to identify the copy.
  • Cume duplication percentage: the percentage of one station’s estimated Cume audience that listened to a second station.
  • Cume persons: the estimated number of different persons who listened to a station for a minimum of five minutes in a quarter hour within a reported daypart. (Cume estimates may also be referred to as cumulative or unduplicated estimates.)
  • Cume rating: the Cume persons audience expressed as a percentage of all persons estimated to be in the specified demographic group.
  • Daypart: a division of the broadcast day into a part of the day.
  • Delivery links: used to manipulate the log, for example to produce logs for multiple time zones, to repeat breaks or time periods, and to produce sub-feeds.
  • Demographic: a population group. There are both standard demographic categories and custom demographic categories.
  • Direct advertiser: an advertiser that purchases an order directly, without using an agency.
  • Direct Response (DR): a type of contract for commercials that asks listeners to “call in” to buy the product. Generally there is a spot cost assigned to each commercial aired.
  • Discrete demographics: uncombined or non-overlapping sex/age groups, such as Men and/or Women 18-24, 25-34, 35,44, as opposed to “target” or aggregate demographics, such as Men and/or Women 18+, 18-34, 18-49, 25-49.
  • DMA (Designated market area): a market or region where the people in that region receive the same radio broadcasts.
  • Dynamic package: a package created “on the fly” on the Proposals or Orders screen using vehicles that have been entered on the contract.
  • Dynamically inserted: a podcast ad that is inserted into a podcast by an Ad Server program, allowing for different listeners listening to the same podcast to possibly hear different ads. The ad is typically stitched together with the podcast audio when the podcast is downloaded.
  • EDI: a type of electronic invoice that can be generated during the Invoice generation process.
  • Effective sample base (ESB): the theoretical sample size used to estimate the sampling error of audience estimates.
  • Engineering links: used to define the engineering feed for satellite transmission.
  • Event number: used with Sports type vehicles and seasons to uniquely identify an event.
  • Exclusive Cume: the number of different persons listening to only one station during a reported daypart.
  • FCC (Federal Communications Commission): a branch of the government charged with ensuring that broadcasters follow the rules and regulations it imposes, such as keeping daily logs of programs aired, allowing public inspection of those logs by citizens, airing a certain amount of public service announcements for free in the public interest, etc.
  • Frequency: The average number of times a single person is projected to hear an advertiser’s commercial across a specific span of time, dates, etc. A research concept used by stations, not networks. (Gross impressions divided by net reach equals frequency). Frequency also refers to an FCC-authorized AM or FM band designation assigned to a city service area and radio station license.
  • Fill: a bonus spot created on the Spots screen using an existing contract as the basis for the spot.
  • Flight: the span of dates and spots defined for a contract line.
  • Generic copy: the copy instructions that apply to all the stations that are not targeted by regional copy for a given set of copy instructions.
  • Ghost avails and spots: cyan colored spots and avails that may be shown on the Spots screen for selling vehicles to indicate the potential for advertiser separation and product protection conflicts to occur depending on the amount of time separating the airing avails.
  • Gross Impressions (GRIMPs): the total times a group of spots are heard by people. Calculated by multiplying the audience for each spot by the number of times it airs.
  • Gross Rating Points (GRPs): the total percentage points of an audience that is reached by a group of spots. Calculated by multiplying the rating for each spot times the number of times it airs.
  • Group Name: used with the Wegener Compel export to link different categories such as the DMA Market and Format with the Wegener Compel definitions.
  • Hidden vehicle: a vehicle that is part of a package on a contract and that is not meant to appear on printed contracts, in order to conceal the names of the specific vehicles that will air the spots.
  • Hold: a type of contract that is still undergoing final approval. Hold inventory is reserved, and revenue for hold contracts is broken out separately on some reports.
  • Hub: a way of grouping stations.
  • Impressions: the total number of exposures to an advertisement. One person can receive multiple exposures, and if that person was exposed to the advertisement five times, that would be five impressions.
  • Insertion order: a contract report that is run by vehicle, with each vehicle shown separately, and that can be sent to a station or vendor to notify them what has been purchased to air.
  • Installment billing: a method of allocating billing to time periods that is independent of when the spots or NTR is ordered. One way of using installment billing is for advance billing, where the entire contract is billed in advance.
  • Inventory: breaks on a vehicle that are sold to advertisers.
  • ISCI: a unique code used to refer to a specific piece of copy.
  • Jobs: in the Counterpoint system, any of the tasks accessed from the buttons on the main screen.
  • Journal entry: a non-revenue transaction used to debit or credit an account.
  • Lists: a screen on the Traffic system made up of numerous sub-screens that are used for configuring options and for entering data such as advertiser and agency information, vehicle options, and so on.
  • Live copy: a commercial where the copy script is read live on air (not a recorded commercial).
  • Live log: a Traffic system feature that allows a user to log spot times as they air.
  • Log vehicle: used to create a single log from multiple vehicles (conventional, airing, and sports vehicles).
  • Log: a list of Traffic system spots and copy for a vehicle and date or date range.
  • Makegood: a spot that has been moved from the ordered specification to another date, time, and/or vehicle. 
  • Market: a division of the country, often the same as a city, except when a group of cities are in close proximity and have similar demographic makeup, such as Minneapolis and St. Paul, which is a single market. (Also called Media Market.)
  • Media Code: a letter designation used with copy inventory.
  • Media Cost: the price paid to present an advertisement. Excludes the cost to create the advertisement.
  • Merchandising and Promotion: used on orders when there is physical merchandise or promotional costs in addition to air time that is being purchased. Examples of Merchandising are flat screen TVs, PCs, iPads. Examples of Promotional materials are t-shirts or coffee mugs with the name of the product, program, or station.
  • Minimum reporting standards (MRS): criteria used to determine which stations qualify to be listed in a market report.
  • Missed reason: the reason why a spot was missed, chosen from a pre-defined list.
  • Missed spot: a spot that is not booked in an avail. A missed has either not aired if it’s in the past, or will not air if it’s in the future and if it’s left as missed and never booked.
  • Moniker: a station nickname such as “The Wave” or “Power 106.”
  • MSA (Metropolitan statistical area): a type of market or region that includes the core of the city.
  • Multicast: a type of arrangement where two or more stations air the same feed at the same time. Multicast stations typically have the same owner and are typically in the same market.
  • Multiple air plays: when an affiliate agrees to air a single spot more than once.
  • Multi-vehicle log: multi-vehicle logs merge spots and program elements from one vehicle into one or more vehicles. This differs from log vehicles because log vehicles combine multiple vehicles into a single log.
  • Net Reach: the number of different persons reached in a given schedule.
  • NTR: non-traditional revenue items that are sold, such as announcer fees, web banners, etc.
  • Order: a finalized contract that contains ordered vehicles, dayparts, rates, spot counts, etc.
  • Outside: a spot that has been moved from the ordered specification to another date, time, and/or vehicle, without notifying the advertiser.
  • Package: a package is a way of grouping vehicles together on a contract. When the contract and invoice is printed, the package and package spots are shown, not the individual vehicles that make up the package. 
  • Per Inquiry (PI): a type of contract similar to a Direct Response contract where the advertiser pays based on the number of responses received, rather than for a specific spot price.
  • Pledge: information about what the station is agreeing to air, as defined on the affiliate agreement.
  • Population: the total number of people in a market.
  • Post Buy Analysis: an analysis provided to an advertiser or agency that lists where and when spots aired across stations for a specific order.
  • Product protection code: a general product category that is defined on an order and that is used to help keep like products scheduled apart from each other.
  • Programming library: used on the Programming screen to define the inventory and programming events for a vehicle.
  • Projections: sales estimates that can be used to gauge possible future business and to follow the progress of negotiations between salespeople and agencies/advertisers.
  • Proposal XML: an export that is used to send a proposal in XML format to an agency.
  • Proposal: a proposed contract that is created by a salesperson and that lists the proposed vehicles and dates that an advertiser or agency may purchase.
  • PSA (Public service announcement): a type of contract used for public service announcements.
  • Qualitative research: a type of research that digs deeper than counting listeners by age group. Instead, it counts people in specific categories or lifestyles of interest to an advertiser. Examples: Number of Hispanic people, number of people planning to buy a new car in the next six months, number of people with swimming pools, etc.
  • Quantitative research: a type of research that counts listeners by age group.
  • Rate card: a list of vehicles and dayparts that are sold, along with default rates. Each year has a new rate card.
  • Rating: of the people who could be listening to a spot, the percentage of people listening to that spot. Calculated by dividing the audience by the population.
  • Reach: how many unique people heard at least one of the spots (not used by networks). This can also be expressed as a percentage, which is called a Reach Percentage.
  • Real package: a real package is more of a way of entering contract lines more quickly in package form, rather than an actual type of package. With a real packages, the package spot count equals the number of hidden line spots that make up the package, and the actual air dates and times are shown on the invoice.
  • Recapturable: also called recaps. Zero cost units intended to make up for under-delivery. Recapturable units can be canceled, or "recaptured".
  • Regional copy: copy that is meant to air only on those stations that meet the criteria defined for an advertiser specific copy region for a specific set of rotation instructions. Also called Split copy.
  • Remnant: a type of contract sold at a lower rate and with a lower priority than standard contracts.
  • Rep vehicle: a type of vehicle that can be entered on an order but that has no inventory and that does not produce logs. Airing information for rep vehicles is returned from the affiliate and updated in the Traffic system on the Post Log screen.
  • Report set: a way of grouping specific Traffic system reports together into a set so that users only see the reports that match what they use the system for. For example, a Traffic user might have the Traffic report set, and a salesperson might have the Sales report set.
  • Research book: the research data that comes from a research provider and that is imported into the Traffic system.
  • Reservation: a type of contract used to reserve inventory in advance for an advertiser.
  • Revenue set: a way of categorizing business for reporting purposes. For example, Upfront and Scatter.
  • Reversal: a Collections screen action used to remove a payment, journal entry, or adjustment that should never have been entered.
  • ROS (run of schedule): a type of delayed pledge (not live) that is pledged to air within a specified date and time range.
  • Season: used with Sports vehicles to group events together into different seasons.
  • Selling to Airing links: used to create associations between avails from selling vehicles with avails from airing vehicles.
  • Selling vehicle: a special type of vehicle that is used for selling, scheduling, and trafficking purposes only. The spots from a selling vehicle actually air on an “airing vehicle”. Selling and airing vehicles are linked together on the Links screen.
  • Service agreement: a special type of agreement that gets exported to X-Digital but is bypassed by most affiliate system reports. Spots from service agreements get marked as posted and compliant automatically when the log gets generated.
  • Short title: a special 15 character name that combines the advertiser and product.
  • Sister station: a group of stations that have the same owner and are generally located in the same market. Defined on the Stations screen, sister stations are also used to group stations together for use with the Marketron system.
  • Spill-in data: extra audience picked up by a station in nearby markets that are not part of the main market.
  • Split copy: copy that is meant to air only on those stations that meet the criteria defined for an advertiser specific copy region for a specific set of rotation instructions. Also called Regional copy.
  • Split fill: used with the Split Networks feature, split fill spots are used to fill those spots that are airing on stations that are not covered by the split network regions that were defined.
  • Split network: a feature that makes it possible to sell different regions of a network to different advertisers. This is different from split copy, in which a single advertiser airs different copy across a network, as split networks involve different advertisers airing at the same time.
  • Sponsorship: a term describing a relationship between a broadcaster and an advertiser that implies that the advertiser is playing an active role in either creating or in some way supporting the program in which its ads are airing. A Sponsorship of a program usually costs more to the advertiser than a Sustaining spot, and usually includes open and/or closing Billboards.
  • Sports vehicle: a type of vehicle with inventory and scheduling based on seasons and event numbers, rather than exact dates and times.
  • Spot: a commercial.
  • Standard line: a contract line that is not a hidden line or not part of a package.
  • Standard package: a pre-created package using a standardized set of vehicles that can be added to a contract.
  • Station ID: a unique number assigned to a station by a data service provider for use with the Station Information import.
  • Sustaining spot: a spot that happens to fall in a particular show, with no Sponsorship relationship.
  • T-Net (Triple Net): gross revenue minus the agency commission, acquisition cost, and percent due other participants.
  • Transfer: a Collections screen action used when cash was applied to the wrong invoice.
  • Unit: a single commercial or spot counts as one unit, regardless of the spot length.
  • Vehicle groups: a way of grouping vehicles together when the accounting revenue for those vehicles should be reported together. The available vehicle groups are format, participant, subtotal, market, research, and sub-company.
  • Vehicle: this term refers to anything that is sold on the Counterpoint Traffic and Affiliate system. It may correspond to a “show” that listeners hear, or it may not, such as with NTR vehicles, selling vehicles, and so on.
  • Virtual package: a type of package where the package spot counts are independent of the total hidden line spot count that makes up the package. With virtual packages, virtual dates and times will appear on the invoice (as opposed to real packages, which show the actual dates and times from the hidden lines on the invoice).
  • Watts: station transmission power.
